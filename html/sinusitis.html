You know the feeling: runny nose, headache, sinus congestion. Is it just a cold? Or something more serious?

Your sinuses are bony, air-filled cavities inside your face and skull. A sinus infection, also called rhinosinusitis, is an inflammation of the soft tissues that line the sinuses.

There are two kinds of sinusitis. A short-term or acute sinus infection can occur after a cold, an allergy attack, or from pollutants in the environment. A long-term or chronic sinus infection is one that lasts longer than 12 weeks.

"I've had constant severe pain behind my eyes. Sometimes throbbing pain in my cheeks and pulling in my ear. It's been going on for a year now."
-CRS sufferer

Symptoms

Physical suffering
  • Headaches
  • Facial pain or pressure
  • Difficulty breathing through nose
  • Nasal discharge that isn't clear
  • Fever
  • Fatigue
  • Upper tooth pain
  • Bad breath
Emotional struggle
  • Headaches
  • Facial pain or pressure
  • Limiting physical activities
  • Taking sick days from work
  • Being too ill to socialize
  • Having problems sleeping
  • Feeling moody

Diagnosis

To diagnose a sinus infection, your doctor will ask about your symptoms and medical history. He or she may tap areas of your face to find out if any sinus openings are tender. Sometimes a CT exam is useful as well.


If your symptoms don't go away with the treatment your family doctor provides, you may be referred to a doctor called an otolaryngologist. This ear, nose, and throat (ENT) specialist will use a nasal endoscope to look inside your nose for blockage. An endoscope is a very thin instrument with a light at the end of it, like a tiny telescope.
